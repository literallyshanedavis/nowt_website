## Why iPad

- **Eyebrow:** Why iPad
- **Headline:** Ideas don't wait till you're back at your desk.

> **Notes:** The original "Built for touch first" was a spec. The first revision "Your fingers are better than a mouse for this" was better — an opinion, not a feature — but it still centred on the *input method*. The real pitch is about *when and where* ideas happen. You're on a shoot, on a walk, sat in a café staring at something interesting — and you can actually do something about it right there. Every desktop node editor (Flora, Krea, ComfyUI) means the idea has to survive the journey home. This headline names that problem without sounding like a complaint.

### Feature 1: Try things where inspiration hits
See something worth generating from? Open your iPad and try it. On location, between meetings, on the sofa. Desktop tools mean the idea has to survive until you're back at your machine. Most don't.

> This is the new lead feature — moved up from being buried across features 3 and 4. The last line ("Most don't") is doing the real work. Everyone knows the feeling of having an idea and losing it before they can act on it. This turns that into a reason to care about the iPad form factor.

### Feature 2: Touch-native editing
Drag nodes with your fingers. Connect them with a swipe. Pinch to zoom. The canvas works how your hands expect — no right-click menus, no browser getting in the way.

> Kept mostly as-was. Still a strong feature, just no longer carrying the section on its own.

### Feature 3: Pencil and camera in the pipeline
Sketch into ControlNet with Apple Pencil. Photograph something straight into a node with the camera. Draw masks, scribble notes on outputs. The hardware you're already holding becomes part of the workflow.

> Combined the old Pencil and Camera features. They were making the same point (iPad hardware = pipeline input) and having them separate made the section feel like a spec sheet. Together they're one compelling idea: the physical device feeds the creative pipeline. "The hardware you're already holding" is better than listing each capability separately.

### Feature 4: Runs without a connection
Smaller models run locally on Apple Silicon. Heavier ones hit the cloud when you've got signal. Either way, you're not tethered to a desk or a GPU tower.

> Reframed from "On-device when it counts" to emphasise the *freedom* angle that supports the section theme. "Not tethered to a desk or a GPU tower" ties back to the headline — you're wherever the idea is, not wherever the hardware is. Dropped "more runs on-device than you'd expect" which was slightly coy.

## How it works

- **Eyebrow:** How it works
- **Headline:** From nowt to summat.

### Step 01: Wire it up
Drop nodes onto the canvas and connect them. Text into image gen, image gen into upscaler, upscaler into output. The connections are type-safe, so you'll know straight away if something doesn't fit.

> The original was correct but abstract ("Connect AI models, inputs, and processors on an infinite canvas"). This version gives a specific, easy-to-picture chain. "You'll know straight away" is more Northern than "you can't wire things wrong" — it's what someone would actually say.

### Step 02: Hit run
Tap the button and watch it go. Each node lights up as it processes — you can see exactly where your pipeline is and what it's doing.

> "The execution engine handles the rest" is developer language for a landing page. "Tap the button and watch it go" is what it actually feels like. Kept the visual of nodes lighting up because that's genuinely appealing.

### Step 03: Keep what works
Save the workflow. Next time, swap out the input image or change a prompt and run it again. Your best creative processes stop being one-offs.

> "Your creative process becomes reusable and refinable" is the kind of sentence that sounds like it should be followed by a trademark symbol. The rewrite says the same thing but in a way that makes you think about *your own* repeated creative tasks.

## General notes on the voice

A few patterns I tried to break throughout:

1. **The three-beat imperative.** "Connect. Build. Create." / "Design. Develop. Deploy." — every AI landing page does this. It reads as confident but it's become wallpaper. I've tried to use flowing sentences instead.

2. **The contrast opener.** "Every X is Y. But nowt is Z." — this positions your product as the exception to a rule you're defining, which always feels like you're building a strawman to knock down. Better to just describe what you do well and let people draw their own comparisons.

3. **Abstract nouns as benefits.** "Full control." "Zero friction." "Total flexibility." These don't create pictures in anyone's head. I've tried to swap them for concrete scenes — working on a train, putting the iPad down, swapping out an image next time.

4. **Technical terms on the landing page.** CoreML and ControlNet are fine for people who know them, but on a landing page you want curiosity first. I've kept ControlNet in the Pencil feature (it's specific and impressive to the target audience) but swapped CoreML for Apple Silicon which everyone recognises.

5. **The Northern voice.** It should feel like someone explaining their project in a relaxed way — not performing Northernness, not putting on a flat cap. "You'll know straight away" instead of "instant validation." "The big stuff still hits the cloud" instead of "computationally intensive models are offloaded." Casual without being sloppy.
